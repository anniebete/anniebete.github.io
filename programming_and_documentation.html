Web Development

iGEM (International Genetically Engineered Machines) Competition

In the late Spring of my sophomore year, Carroll High School and Air Force Research Laboratories (AFRL) collaborated to create its inagural iGEM team. iGEM is a competition where high school, undergraduate, and overgraduate teams brainstorm, design, and conduct research on a synthetic biology project. Outside of the research, a large part of the project involves collaborating with other iGEM teams and reaching out to people who might use your project. In the fall, teams travel to Boston, MA to present their project to experts at the International Jamboree.

Each team must also create a wiki page on the iGEM server (MediaWiki platform) to document their entire project. Although they are called a "wiki page", in reality they are full websites. Not only is the wiki page a medal requirement, but it is the way that teams document all of their work for the current (and future) teams, the judges, and the public.

2017

When our team first began, no one had any website experience, including myself. I immediately started playing around with the code, but was unable to change anything besides fonts and colors. I took a Codecademy HTML/CSS course to familiarize myself with the basics, then began to try to design the website. Fortunately, two other people on the team were interested in helping with the design and, between the three of us, we were able to create a basic wiki, which you can access by clicking the image below.

2018

Although our 2017 wiki accomplished what we needed it to do and had a clean design, we knew that it was missing some elements, especially making it mobile-friendly. Going into 2018, our big wiki goal was to make the page responsive.

In 2018 there were only two of us focusing on the wiki design. We looked into some different ways of doing this and ultimately favored using Bootstrap. We tried this in 2017, but due to an iGEM rule that all content must be hosted on the iGEM server, we were unable to link to external pages. Linking to stylesheets on the iGEM server was not commonly done, but with the help of some other teams we were finally able to figure out how to make it work.

This marked a turning point for our wiki, which can be accessed below. Now that we had more experience, our design also improved. Our team's decision to have consistant branding made the wiki more cohesive. Finally, we started to work more design into it. I created the Airplane Design Collection to use as a main design element.

2019

By 2019, both of the other team members who helped with the wiki had graduated, so I mainly played around with the design on my own, and got feedback from my team along the way. I also felt the most confident this year. The struggles of the past two years set me up so that I knew what needed to be done and could spend more time designing and less time debugging.

It also marked my last year on the team and I knew that I wanted to save others as much trouble as possible. I decided to lead an iGEM collaboration to teach others how to put together a wiki and outline their options. I created a written guide, YouTube series, and GitHub repositories to effectively communicate the development process. I also provided myself as a resource to teams to try to help them debug their code.

To truly make this successful, though, I knew that I needed the help of other teams. I reached out to at least 200 teams (I stopped tracking at that point) and had over 30 teams collaborate by writing content and giving feedback on what had been written. We could see that it was successful by the feedback we were getting. One of the most memorable was when someone had the Jamboree (competition) approached us after our presentation to tell us how she wouldn't have been able to get Bootstrap to work without our help. This meant a lot, since it was something I had stuggled with for the first two years.
